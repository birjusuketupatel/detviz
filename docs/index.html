WHAT IS THE DETERMINANT?

The determinant of a matrix is equal to the oriented volume of the shape spanned by its column vectors. To see why this is true, and how it generalizes to n-by-n matrices, lets start in 2 dimensions. Consider the matrix below.

For each column vector in the matrix, the first row is to the x-coordinate, and the second row is the y-coordinate. A parallelogram is formed by connecting the origin, \(a_1\), \(a_2\), and \(a_1 + a_2\). This parallelogram has some area \(A(a_1, a_2)\).

Now we will transform the parallelogram in a way that preserves its area. Plot a line starting at the origin extending in the direction of \(a_1\). From points \(a_2\) and \(a_1 + a_2\), draw lines that intersect the previous line at a 90 degree angle.

Consider the right triangles \(OBD\) and \(ACE\). Their hypotenuses \(\overline{OB}\) and \(\overline{AC}\) are the same length because opposite sides of a parallelogram have equal length. Also, legs \(\overline{BD}\) and \(\overline{CE}\) have equal length because they bisect parallel lines at the same angle. So, triangles \(OBD\) and \(ACE\) are congruent and have the same area.

Consider the parallelogram \(OACB\) and the rectangle \(BCED\). By cutting triangle \(OBD\) from the parallelogram and pasting triangle \(ACE\), we can form the rectangle. So \(A(Rectangle) = A(Parallelogram) + A(ACE) - A(OBD)\). But we know \(A(ACE) = A(OBD)\). Therefore, \(A(Rectangle)=A(Parallelogram)\).
